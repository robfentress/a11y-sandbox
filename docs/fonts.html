Font Accessibility

A Note on Terminology

If you are unsure what any of the terms used below mean, refer to the following page: Anatomy of a Character.

General Guidance

  • Font should use consistent stroke width. Any weight changes within characters should be subtle.
  • Letters should have large counterforms or "counters"
  • Letters should have open apertures
  • Letters should have pronounced ascenders and descenders
  • Letters should have wider horizontal proportions
  • Letters should have ample lowercase x-heights
  • Font should have distinct forms for each character (such as tails on the lowercase letters "t" and "j")
  • Font should use extended horizontal strokes for certain letterforms (such as the arm of the lowercase letter "r" or the crossbar of the lowercase letter "t")
  • You should generally avoid scripts and decorative typestyles
  • Font should not use excessively light or bold weighting, but lighter is probably better. Stroke thickness of around 18% of x-height best.
  • Sans serif, roman (as opposed to italic or oblique), and monospaced fonts are preferable for dyslexia
  • If font has serifs, they should be short, bracketed, and not distracting
  • If font is sans serif, ensure sufficient kerning
  • Avoid fonts with all capitalized letters

Situational factors

  • Sans serif better for numerals
  • When in cramped quarters, using fonts with condensed proportions may provide a more legible alternative, when the alternative is reducing font size
  • Sans serif better for smaller font sizes

Heuristic Testing for Font Legibility

As a simple heuristic spot check, to see how your choices might affect people with low visual acuity, try this (note, though, that people with dyslexia or other cognitive disabilities may also be affected by font choices):

Go to TypeKits and for each font, at 16 points, enter the following character set:

qwertyuiopasdfghjklzxcvbnm
QWERTYUIOPASDFGHJKLZXCVBNM
1234567890`~!@#$%^&*()-_=+[]\{ }|;’:",./<>?

Using the NoCoffee Vision Simulator for Chrome, set "Blur (low acuity)" to 3, and see how well each one holds up in terms of ability to distinguish between characters, paying particular attention to filled in counters.

In looking for distinctions between letters, consider the difference between the following:

  • I (uppercase i -- India), l (lowercase el -- Lima) and 1 (one),
  • 1 (one), and 7 (seven)
  • ! (exclamation point), and i (lowercase i -- India)
  • B (uppercase bee -- Bravo), 8 (eight), and 9 (nine)
  • 3 (three), and 8 (eight)
  • c (lowercase cee -- Charlie), and o (lowercase o -- Oscar)
  • d (lowercase dee -- Delta), a (lowercase a), o (lowercase o -- Oscar), and g (lowercase gee -- Golf)
  • 0 (zero), O (uppercase o -- Oscar)
  • g (lowercase gee -- Golf), q (lowercase cue -- Quebec), and p (lowercase pee -- Papa)
  • G (uppercase gee -- Golf), and & (ampersand)
  • 5 (five), and 6 (six)
  • 6 (six), and G (uppercase gee -- Golf)
  • 5 (five), and S (uppercase ess -- Sierra)
  • A (uppercase a -- Alpha), R (uppercase ar -- Romeo)
